Case Study UX/UI Design
BRANCHOUT
Social platform design zero to beta launch
Role
UX/UI Lead & Researcher
Timeline
Jan 2024 – Present
Team
Cross-functional
Platform
Mobile & Web
01
PROBLEM
STOPPING PLANS FROM FALLING THROUGH

In a recent survey by the Institute of Politics at Harvard Kennedy School, just 17% of U.S. adults under 30 report feeling deeply connected to at least one community. While many solutions exist on the market, they often fall short of actually fostering connections to communities and events among young adults.

Recognizing these statistics, we saw an opportunity to help users find the communities they love and foster real social connection.

02
RESEARCH
UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

We conducted interviews and surveys with over 100 college students across Southern California to fully understand the problem of lack of connection among young adults.

Reach Out Fatigue
Many people expressed frustration with always being the ones to initiate contact, feeling that the other party had a lack of interest.
Last Minute Cancellations
Building up excitement around a plan only for it to fall through last minute was one of the most common frustrations we heard.
Hindsight FOMO
Many users reported events in their area passed them by unnoticed due to the noise of social media platforms where events get spread.
03
IDEATION
EARLY IDEATION

Knowing we wanted to create a platform connecting users with events and activities they were interested in, we started exploring several iterations of how best to deliver on that. We decided a home screen built around a calendar would give users the clearest visual representation of their day.

Branchout Design System Color Palette & Typography
EARLY DRAFTS

After figuring out the basics of the home screen, we explored several different layout ideas.

Early Homepage Iterations Home Screen Without Invite Option
USER TESTING

After building the homepage around a calendar design, we began user testing letting small groups of 10–15 people use the app and provide feedback on how it looked, felt, and any friction they encountered.

Lack of Modern Look
A common theme was that the design lacked innovation users described it as bland in layout and visual style.
Complicated Plan Creation
Users had difficulty creating plans. They integrated well into the calendar once made, but getting there created too much friction.
Visual Clutter
Almost everyone noted that events being constantly surfaced on the homepage caused fatigue and made them not want to spend time in the app.
04
SOLUTION
THE SCALABLE SOLUTION

After user testing feedback, we updated the design to feel more modern, use tones easier on the eyes, and tackled the friction in plan creation head-on.

Sleek gradient patterns give variety of color
AI suggests activities with friends based on your schedule
Activity creation available manually all through the homepage
Swipe to plan feature added
Day/night cycle added when expanding calendar day view
Final Design AI Suggestions & Gradient System

Case Study UX Research & UI Redesign
RIVVY.IO
Usability research and UI redesign to reduce friction and improve conversion
Role
UX Designer
Timeline
Aug – Sep 2025
Type
Freelance Project
Platform
Web
01
OVERVIEW
THE BRIEF

Rivvy came to me with a conversion problem. Users were landing on the platform but dropping off before completing key actions. The goal was to identify exactly where and why friction was occurring, and redesign the affected UI to reduce drop-off and improve overall user flow.

This was a research-first engagement no assumptions, no guessing. Every design decision made during the redesign was grounded in what real users told us through testing.

02
RESEARCH
FINDING THE FRICTION

I led end-to-end usability testing sessions on the live Rivvy platform, recruiting participants and running structured sessions to observe how real users moved through the product. Rather than relying on heuristics alone, I prioritized watching users attempt real tasks without guidance letting behavior surface the problems.

Unclear Entry Points
Users consistently hesitated at key action points, unsure what the next step was or what would happen if they clicked.
Visual Hierarchy Issues
Important CTAs were competing with secondary content for attention, causing users to scan past what they should have been acting on.
Flow Interruptions
Several steps in the conversion flow required information or decisions that users weren't prepared for, causing drop-off mid-task.

Usability Testing Key Friction Points Identified
03
REDESIGN
THE REDESIGN

With the pain points clearly defined, I moved into redesign. Working in Photoshop and InDesign, I produced updated UI mock-ups that directly addressed each identified friction point prioritizing clarity, visual hierarchy, and a smoother path through the conversion flow.

Simplified entry points with clearer CTA language and visual weight
Restructured visual hierarchy to guide attention toward primary actions
Reduced mid-flow decision points to minimize drop-off moments
Improved information density less clutter, more clarity at each step
[ Add image: rivvy-redesign.png before/after UI mock-ups ]
UI Redesign Before & After Key Screens
04
OUTCOME
WHAT CHANGED

The redesigned screens addressed every friction point surfaced during testing. The updated flow reduced the number of decision points users had to navigate, clarified the visual hierarchy at each stage, and made the primary conversion actions significantly more prominent.

The process demonstrated what research-led design actually looks like in practice not designing from assumptions, but from evidence. Every pixel in the redesign had a reason behind it rooted in something a real user showed us.

Case Study UI Design & Visual Systems
CHRONOMANCY
Full UI system design for an indie game concept sketches through production screens
Role
UI Designer
Timeline
Feb – Sep 2025
Tools
Adobe XD & Illustrator
Platform
PC / Multi-screen
01
OVERVIEW
THE PROJECT

Chronomancy is an indie game built by an independent development team. I was brought on as UI Designer to own the full visual interface building a cohesive UI system from early concept sketches through to production-ready screens that shipped with the game.

The challenge with game UI is that it has to do two things simultaneously: disappear when the player is immersed, and communicate clearly when they need information fast. Every design decision had to serve both of those masters.

02
CONCEPT
EARLY CONCEPTS

The process started with rough concept sketches exploring visual language, tone, and layout before committing to anything in the tool. Game UI has a very different vocabulary from product UI, so establishing the aesthetic direction early was critical to staying consistent through implementation.

I explored multiple directions before landing on an approach that matched the game's theme and felt native to the experience rather than bolted on.

Early Concept Sketches — Visual Language Exploration
03
SYSTEM
BUILDING THE UI SYSTEM

Once the visual direction was established, I moved into Adobe XD and Illustrator to build out the full UI system. This meant creating reusable components, defining the type system, establishing the color language, and designing every interactive state all documented clearly enough that developers could implement without ambiguity.

Designed wireframes and interactive prototypes for all core game screens
Built reusable component library covering HUD elements, menus, and overlays
Established responsive layouts that held across multiple screen sizes
Collaborated directly with developers to ensure pixel-accurate implementation
Maintained visual consistency and usability from concept through final build
UI Design Stage — Components, Menus & HUD Elements
04
FINAL
PRODUCTION SCREENS

The final screens shipped with the game. The UI had to hold up under real play conditions working across screen sizes, reading clearly in motion, and staying out of the way when the player didn't need it. Close collaboration with the development team throughout ensured nothing got lost between design and build.
